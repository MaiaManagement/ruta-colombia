Let's be straight with you: opening a bank account in Colombia as a foreigner is entirely doable, but it will test your patience at least once. The system is a mix of old-school bureaucracy and surprisingly modern fintech, and knowing which path to take before you walk into a branch will save you hours of queueing and a mild existential crisis.

Here's everything you actually need to know.

## What Documents Do You Need?

This is where most foreigners get tripped up. The document you hold determines which doors open for you.

**Cédula de Extranjería (CE)** — This is the holy grail. It's the Colombian foreign ID card issued by Migración Colombia once you have a resident or long-stay visa (Migrant or Resident category). With a cédula de extranjería, you can open a full current account (*cuenta corriente*) or savings account (*cuenta de ahorros*) at virtually any bank, apply for credit, and be treated more or less like a local customer. Getting your CE should be a priority if you're staying longer than a year.

**Passport only** — If you're on a tourist visa or haven't yet obtained your cédula, your options narrow considerably. A handful of banks and most digital fintechs will still work with you, but traditional full-service accounts are harder to access. Nequi, Nu Colombia, and RappiPay are your best bets here.

Regardless of what you hold, expect to provide:

- Your passport or cédula de extranjería (original, not a photocopy)
- Proof of address in Colombia (*comprobante de domicilio*) — a utility bill, lease agreement, or even a signed letter from your landlord works
- Your RUT (tax registration number from the DIAN) — some banks require this, others don't, but it's worth getting sorted. You can register online at dian.gov.co
- Proof of income or economic activity — a payslip, freelance contracts, or a letter explaining your income source. Colombian banks are paranoid about money laundering compliance (known locally as SARLAFT), so they want to know where your money comes from

## The Best Banks for Foreigners in 2026

### Bancolombia

The largest bank in Colombia and the one most expats end up with eventually. Bancolombia has decent English-language support on its app and a relatively straightforward account-opening process once you have your cédula. Their savings account (*Cuenta de Ahorros Bancolombia*) has no minimum balance requirement in its basic form, but you'll pay a monthly fee of around 14,000–18,000 COP for account management plus transaction fees. Their ATM network is the most extensive in the country — you'll find a Bancolombia cajero in most towns.

The downside: in-branch service can be glacially slow. If you're in Bogotá, the branches around El Poblado in Medellín or Zona Rosa tend to have slightly shorter queues and staff more used to dealing with foreigners.

### Davivienda

Davivienda is a solid second option and some expats find their staff more willing to work with passport-only applicants depending on the branch manager's discretion (yes, it really does vary by branch). Monthly fees sit around 12,000–16,000 COP. Their app is functional if not exactly beautiful, and they have a decent ATM footprint.

### Nu Colombia (Nubank)

This is the recommendation we make most often to newly arrived foreigners, especially those not yet holding a cédula. Nu Colombia is a fully digital bank — no branches, no queues, no suffering. You can open an account with just your passport and a selfie verification process done entirely on your phone. There are **zero monthly fees**, no minimum balance, and the app is genuinely excellent.

The main limitation is that Nu operates as a credit/debit product hybrid in Colombia. Their debit card works for most purchases and online payments, but cash withdrawals from ATMs carry a fee (around 4,500–6,000 COP per withdrawal after the first free one per month). For day-to-day spending and receiving transfers, though, it's hard to beat.

### Nequi

Technically a digital wallet rather than a full bank account (it's Bancolombia's fintech spin-off), Nequi deserves a mention because it's insanely popular and practical. You can open a Nequi account with just your passport, receive transfers instantly, pay at thousands of merchants via QR code, and withdraw cash at Bancolombia ATMs and Éxito supermarkets. There are no monthly fees. 

The catch: there are daily transaction limits (around 3,000,000 COP per day) and you can't receive international wire transfers directly. But as a secondary wallet for daily life — buying a *tinto* at a café in Laureles, splitting a meal on a night out in Getsemaní, paying your local *droguería* — Nequi is excellent.

### RappiPay

If you use Rappi for food delivery anyway (and in Colombia, most people do), RappiPay is worth activating. It functions similarly to Nequi — digital wallet, QR payments, no monthly fee — and can be set up with passport-level identification. Useful as a backup payment method.

## Step-by-Step: Opening a Traditional Bank Account

1. **Get your documents in order** — CE or passport, comprobante de domicilio, RUT if you have it, and a clear explanation of your income source
2. **Choose your bank and book an appointment** — Don't just walk into a Bancolombia branch on a Monday morning. Use their website or app to schedule a *cita* (appointment). Walk-ins at major branches in Bogotá's Centro Internacional or Medellín's El Centro can mean waits of 90 minutes or more
3. **At the branch, ask specifically for a cuenta de ahorros** — This is easier to open than a current account and has fewer requirements
4. **Complete the SARLAFT form** — This is the compliance paperwork asking about your economic activity. Be honest and specific. Vague answers cause delays
5. **Fund the account** — Some banks require a minimum opening deposit of around 50,000–100,000 COP
6. **Activate your card and app** — Your physical debit card usually arrives within 5–10 business days. In the meantime, you'll have a virtual card number for online use

## Getting Money Into Colombia: Transfer Options

Once your account is open, you need to actually get money into it. Here are your real options:

**Wise** — The go-to for most expats. Transfers from USD, EUR, or GBP to COP via Wise are fast (usually same-day), transparent on fees, and use the mid-market exchange rate. You can send directly to a Colombian bank account. Fees typically run 0.5–1.5% depending on the amount and currency.

**Remitly** — Strong for USD-to-COP transfers, particularly for larger amounts. The promotional rates on first transfers are genuinely competitive. Worth comparing with Wise for each transfer.

**Western Union / Efecty** — Still used, particularly for cash pickups. If you're sending money to someone without a bank account, Efecty (Western Union's Colombian partner) has locations in every neighbourhood. Fees are higher and rates less favourable, but it's widely accessible.

**Crypto** — More expats are using USDT or USDC via exchanges like Binance P2P to convert dollars to pesos at near-black-market rates. This sits in a legal grey area in Colombia and the DIAN is increasingly paying attention to crypto flows. Use with awareness of the implications.

## Can Foreigners Get a Credit Card?

The honest answer: it's difficult without a solid Colombian credit history. Colombian credit scoring works through a bureau called DataCrédito, and if you've just arrived, you have no history there.

Your best realistic route is to open a savings account first, maintain it in good standing for 6–12 months, and then apply for a low-limit credit card with the same bank. Bancolombia and Davivienda both offer secured credit card options where you deposit a guarantee amount (typically 500,000–2,000,000 COP) which becomes your credit limit.

Nu Colombia has also started offering credit products to long-standing customers, and because their onboarding is frictionless, this can be a faster path to your first Colombian credit card.

## The 4x1000 Tax: What It Is and How to Minimise It

The *gravamen a los movimientos financieros* — universally called the cuatro por mil — is a 0.4% tax on most financial transactions: withdrawals, transfers, payments. It sounds small, but it adds up.

On a 1,000,000 COP withdrawal, you'll pay 4,000 COP in tax. On a 10,000,000 COP transfer, that's 40,000 COP gone. The tax is collected automatically by your bank.

You're allowed to designate **one savings account** per bank as exempt from the 4x1000 on the first 350 UVT of transactions per month (roughly 16,500,000 COP in 2026 terms). Make sure you ask your bank to mark your primary account as the exenta account — it's not automatic at every institution.

Practical tip: minimise the number of transfers you make. Withdraw larger amounts less frequently, rather than making multiple small withdrawals.

## Common Frustrations (and How to Handle Them)

**The branch that won't open your account** — Colombian banking has infuriating inconsistency. One branch refuses a passport-only application; another one in the same city approves it. If you're rejected, try a different branch before giving up. Smaller branches in residential neighbourhoods are often more flexible than major commercial ones.

**Blocked transactions on international cards** — Colombian banks flag foreign-issued cards frequently for security. If your Wise or UK card stops working, call the bank's fraud line immediately or use the app to confirm the transaction.

**ATM limits** — Most banks cap single ATM withdrawals at 400,000–600,000 COP, and daily limits sit around 1,200,000–2,000,000 COP. Plan cash needs in advance, especially if you're heading somewhere rural.

**App verification loops** — Colombian banking apps love to send SMS verification codes to Colombian numbers. Get a local SIM (Claro and Tigo have plans from around 25,000 COP/month) before you try to set up banking apps. Trying to do it on a foreign number is a special kind of torture.

The Colombian banking system isn't perfect, but once you're set up properly — ideally with a cédula, a Nu or Nequi account for daily spending, and a traditional account for receiving larger transfers — it works reliably enough. The key is getting the paperwork right the first time and not underestimating the bureaucracy. Go prepared, go patient, and you'll be fine.
