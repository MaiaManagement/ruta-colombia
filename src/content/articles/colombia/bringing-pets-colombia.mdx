Let's be honest: moving to Colombia is complicated enough without throwing a anxious Labrador or a sulking cat into the mix. But thousands of expats do it every year, and once you've navigated the paperwork, pet life here is genuinely good — green parks, affordable vets, and a culture that, while not quite as dog-obsessed as, say, Buenos Aires, is warming up fast. Here's everything you actually need to know.

## ICA Requirements: The Paperwork That Matters

Colombia's agricultural authority, the **ICA** (Instituto Colombiano Agropecuario), controls what animals enter the country. Don't skip or fudge this — ICA officers at the airport are not flexible, and a missing stamp can mean your pet goes straight into quarantine or gets sent back on the next flight.

For dogs and cats, you'll need:

**1. A valid health certificate** issued by an accredited vet in your home country, dated no more than **10 days before departure**. This is the document that trips most people up — ten days sounds like plenty until you're juggling airline check-in and international travel.

**2. Proof of vaccinations**, specifically:
- Rabies (must be administered at least 30 days before travel, but no more than 12 months prior)
- Distemper, parvovirus, hepatitis (dogs)
- Feline panleukopenia, rhinotracheitis, calicivirus (cats)

**3. A CVI (Certificate of Veterinary Inspection)**, which is essentially the official government-endorsed version of your health certificate. In the UK, this means getting an **AHC (Animal Health Certificate)** issued by an Official Veterinarian (OV). In the US, it's a USDA-endorsed health certificate. Your regular vet can prepare the underlying document, but the endorsement has to come from the relevant government body — in the UK that's APHA, in the US it's the USDA APHIS office. Budget 2–3 weeks for this process and don't leave it to the last minute.

**4. Microchip** — your pet must be microchipped to ISO standard 11784/11785 before the rabies vaccination is administered. If the chip comes after the vaccine, the timeline doesn't count. Check this with your vet explicitly.

There is **no mandatory quarantine** for pets entering Colombia from most countries, provided your paperwork is in order. That's the good news.

## Airline Policies: Cabin vs Cargo

This varies enormously by airline and route, so always confirm directly before booking.

**In-cabin** (typically for pets under 8–10 kg including carrier): Avianca allows small pets in-cabin on domestic Colombian routes and some international ones. On transatlantic flights, you're almost certainly looking at cargo. LATAM, Copa, and Air France all have slightly different weight limits and carrier dimension rules — check each airline's specific policy because it changes.

**Cargo (checked baggage hold)**: For larger dogs, this is the only option. It sounds alarming but it's a pressurised, temperature-controlled environment. That said, most airlines won't accept pets in cargo during summer months when tarmac temperatures are extreme, and brachycephalic breeds (pugs, French bulldogs, Persian cats) face restrictions year-round due to breathing risks. If you have one of these dogs, start researching early — some airlines flat-out refuse them.

Costs vary widely: in-cabin fees run roughly **$50–$150 USD** per flight. Cargo fees for a large dog can hit **$200–$400 USD** or more depending on the crate size and route.

## Step-by-Step Timeline

**12+ weeks before departure**
- Microchip your pet if not already done
- Start (or update) vaccination schedule — rabies must be at least 30 days before travel

**8 weeks before**
- Book your flight and confirm the airline's pet policy in writing
- Identify an Official Veterinarian or USDA-accredited vet in your area
- Order or buy an airline-approved carrier and start crate training

**2–3 weeks before**
- Visit your vet for the health certificate preparation
- Submit paperwork to APHA/USDA for government endorsement — this takes 5–10 business days typically

**7–10 days before**
- Collect the endorsed CVI/AHC (check dates carefully — the 10-day clock starts from the vet signature)
- Confirm your booking with the airline and check in any pet-specific requirements

**Day of travel**
- Arrive at the airport early — check-in with a pet takes longer than you'd expect
- Carry all documents in a clearly labelled folder: CVI, vaccination records, microchip documentation

## Arriving at the Airport: What Happens at Customs

When you land at El Dorado (Bogotá), José María Córdova (Medellín/Rionegro), or another international airport, you'll go through the standard customs process and then present your pet's documents to the ICA inspection point. In Bogotá this is usually a designated area near the agricultural inspection desk after baggage collection.

An ICA official will review your CVI, check the microchip number against your documents, and verify vaccination dates. If everything checks out, they'll stamp your paperwork and you're free to go. The whole process typically takes 20–45 minutes if your documents are complete.

Keep all original documents — you may need them again when taking your pet out of Colombia.

## Finding a Pet-Friendly Apartment

Here's the uncomfortable truth: a significant number of Colombian landlords (*arrendadores*) won't rent to pet owners. The word you'll see constantly in listings is "*no mascotas*" — no pets. In cities like Medellín and Bogotá, this rules out a frustrating chunk of otherwise good apartments.

**Where to look:**
- **Finca Raíz** (finca raiz.com.co) — Colombia's main property portal. Filter listings and look for ones that specify "*mascotas permitidas*"
- **Facebook Marketplace** and local Facebook groups (search "arriendo Medellín mascotas" or similar) — private landlords here are often more flexible than agencies
- **Airbnb medium-term rentals** — many superhosts accommodate pets; message them directly and be upfront

**Tips for negotiating:**
- Offer one extra month's deposit (*depósito*) — this reassures landlords worried about damage
- Bring a photo of your pet and be specific: "She's 6kg, calm, house-trained, doesn't bark"
- Offer to pay for professional cleaning at the end of the tenancy in writing
- Neighbourhoods with more expat-friendly housing stock — El Poblado and Laureles in Medellín, Chapinero and Usaquén in Bogotá, Bocagrande in Cartagena — tend to have more flexible landlords, though rents are higher

Budget for apartments in El Poblado: expect **2,500,000–4,500,000 COP/month** for a decent one-bedroom. Laureles runs slightly cheaper at **1,800,000–3,200,000 COP** and is often more genuinely residential and dog-friendly in feel.

## Veterinary Care in Colombia

Colombian vet care is good and dramatically cheaper than North America or Western Europe. In a major city, you'll find well-equipped clinics with competent vets — many trained abroad or with strong international curricula.

**Typical costs in 2026:**
- Routine consultation: **35,000–70,000 COP**
- Annual vaccination package (dogs): **80,000–150,000 COP**
- Spay/neuter: **250,000–600,000 COP** depending on size and clinic
- Emergency consultation (out of hours): **150,000–300,000 COP** plus treatment
- Dental cleaning under anaesthesia: **400,000–900,000 COP**

In Medellín, **Clínica Veterinaria Laureles** on Carrera 76 and **VetLine** in El Poblado are popular with expats. In Bogotá, look at clinics in Chapinero and Usaquén. For emergencies, **Clínica Veterinaria 24 Horas** branches exist in both cities.

Pet insurance isn't deeply established in Colombia yet, but some Colombian insurers and international providers (Trupanion covers Colombia for US/Canadian policyholders) offer plans worth investigating.

## Parks, Beaches, and Pet-Friendly Spots

Medellín's **Parque El Poblado** and **Parque Laureles** are both popular morning dog-walking spots. **Parque Arví**, accessible via the Metrocable, is a vast green space where dogs are welcome on leads. The **ciclovía** on Sundays — when major roads close to traffic — is great for longer walks.

In Bogotá, **Parque de la 93** in Chicó and **Parque El Virrey** in Usaquén are the go-to spots. The **ciclovía** here is enormous and very dog-friendly.

For cafés, the pet-friendly culture is growing. In Medellín, several spots in **Parque Bello Horizonte** and along **Avenida El Poblado** have outdoor terraces that welcome dogs. Ask "*¿Aceptan mascotas?*" — most honest places will tell you upfront.

Beaches in Colombia's Caribbean coast (Cartagena, Santa Marta) technically allow dogs, though some organised beach clubs don't. The beaches around **Palomino** in the Sierra Nevada foothills are more relaxed about it.

## Dog Walkers, Pet Sitters, and Apps

**Guau** is the Colombian equivalent of Rover — you can find dog walkers, pet sitters, and dog boarding through the app. Rates typically run **25,000–45,000 COP for a 30-minute walk**, and **60,000–100,000 COP per night** for boarding in someone's home. Reviews are generally reliable and the platform has decent coverage in Medellín and Bogotá.

**Rover** itself operates in Colombia's major cities too. WhatsApp groups in local expat communities (search Facebook for "Expats in Medellín" or "Bogotá Expats") are also gold for personal recommendations.

## Pet Food and Supplies

You'll find **Royal Canin, Hills Science Diet, Purina Pro Plan**, and several decent Colombian brands in supermarkets and specialist pet shops. **Alkosto**, **Éxito**, and **Jumbo** all stock a reasonable range. Specialist pet shops (*veterinarias* often double as pet supply stores) carry the fuller range.

Expect to pay **85,000–160,000 COP** for a 15kg bag of mid-range dry dog food. Specialist or veterinary prescription diets are available but pricier and less consistently stocked — if your pet is on something specific, bring a supply and source it through a vet clinic or importation service once you're settled.

## Taking Your Pet Out of Colombia

If you're leaving Colombia with your pet, you'll need an **ICA export health certificate**, issued by a Colombian Official Veterinarian and endorsed by ICA — similar in concept to what you needed coming in. Start this process at least **3–4 weeks before departure**.

Requirements vary by destination: returning to the EU or UK requires the Colombian vet to complete documentation that aligns with import standards there, which means your vet needs to know what they're doing. Larger cities have vets experienced in this; ask specifically whether they've handled export documentation for your destination country.

## Adopting a Pet in Colombia

If you're open to it, adopting locally is genuinely worth considering. Colombia has a huge stray animal population (*animales callejeros*), and rescue organisations do extraordinary work with limited resources.

In Medellín, **Amigos de los Animales** and **Huellitas con Amor** regularly have dogs and cats ready for adoption. In Bogotá, check **Proyecto ACAM** and the city's own adoption programmes. Adoption fees typically run **80,000–200,000 COP** and usually include basic vaccinations and microchipping.

The practicalities of later taking an adopted Colombian pet to another country are the same as with any pet — you'll need to build their vaccination and microchip record from scratch, which takes time. If you're planning to leave Colombia within 6–12 months, factor this into your timeline carefully.

---

The bottom line: bringing a pet to Colombia is absolutely doable, it just requires you to be organised well in advance and to treat the ICA paperwork with the same seriousness you'd give your own visa. Once you're here, the combination of affordable vet care, green space, and a growing pet-friendly culture makes it a genuinely comfortable country in which to share your life with an animal.
