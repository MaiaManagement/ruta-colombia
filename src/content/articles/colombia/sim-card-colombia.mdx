Getting a working SIM card sorted within your first 48 hours in Colombia should be near the top of your list — above finding a good bandeja paisa, probably below finding somewhere to sleep. Without local data, navigating apps, booking rides, and generally functioning as a human being in a new city becomes a lot harder. Here's everything you need to know.

## The Three Main Carriers: Claro, Movistar, and Tigo

Colombia's mobile market is dominated by three operators, and each has its strengths depending on where you're spending most of your time.

**Claro** is the coverage king. If you're travelling outside the major cities — heading into the coffee region, spending time on the Caribbean coast, or venturing into less-visited departments — Claro is almost always the safest bet. Their 4G network reaches places the other two simply don't bother with. In cities, they're strong across the board.

**Tigo** performs best in urban centres and is particularly solid in Medellín, Barranquilla, and Cali. Their data packages are often the most competitive on price, and they've invested heavily in 5G infrastructure in Bogotá and Medellín. Worth considering if you're staying put in a major city.

**Movistar** (Telefónica's Colombian arm) tends to lag behind the other two in both coverage and value, though they've improved meaningfully in recent years. Their postpaid plans come with some solid international roaming add-ons, which can be useful if you're moving between Colombia and other Latin American countries frequently.

For most newcomers: **start with Claro if you're travelling around the country, Tigo if you're parking yourself in Medellín or Bogotá and want to squeeze the most data for your money.**

## Prepaid vs Postpaid (Prepago vs Pospago)

As a tourist or recent arrival, you'll almost certainly be going **prepago** — prepaid. It requires no credit check, no Colombian cédula (national ID), and you can get sorted in under 20 minutes.

**Prepago** works on a recharge model. You buy a SIM, load credit or activate a data package (*paquete* or *combo*), and use it until it runs out. Most packages last 30 days. Simple, no commitment.

**Pospago** (postpaid) is a monthly contract with a set bill at the end of the month. To get one, you'll need a cédula de extranjería (foreign resident ID) or a Colombian cédula — a passport alone won't cut it with most carriers. You'll also need to pass a basic credit assessment. If you're planning to stay in Colombia long-term and have your residency documents sorted, postpaid plans offer better value per gigabyte and usually include device financing options if you need a new phone.

## Where to Buy a SIM Card

**Official carrier stores** are absolutely the way to go. Yes, you'll see people selling SIM cards on street corners and in small tiendas around bus terminals — and while most of those are legitimate cards, you have zero recourse if something goes wrong and no help activating the plan.

In Bogotá, every major shopping centre (Centro Comercial Andino, Gran Estación, El Retiro, Unicentro) has Claro, Tigo, and Movistar stores. In Medellín, head to any El Tesoro, Santafé, or Viva stores, or go directly to the carrier stores along Avenida El Poblado or in the El Centro area around Parque Berrío. In Cartagena, the Claro store on Avenida Venezuela near the Bocagrande entrance is reliable and accustomed to dealing with foreigners.

SIM cards themselves are either free or cost between **2,000 and 5,000 COP** at official stores. Don't pay more than that.

## What You'll Need

Bring your **passport**. That's genuinely all you need as a tourist. Staff will photocopy it (or photograph it on their phone — very normal) and register the SIM in your name, which is required by Colombian law under the *registro de usuarios* system.

If you're a resident with a cédula de extranjería, bring that instead — it'll open up postpaid options and sometimes better promotional rates on prepaid packages too.

Your phone needs to be **unlocked** to use a local SIM. If you bought it through a carrier back home, double-check this before you travel. Most modern flagship phones are sold unlocked, but budget Android devices bought through networks often aren't.

## Best Prepaid Data Packages and Current Prices

Prices shift fairly regularly as carriers compete, but here's a realistic picture of what you'll find in 2026:

**Claro Prepago:**
- 10 GB data + unlimited WhatsApp: around **19,900 COP/month**
- 25 GB data + social media unlimited: around **34,900 COP/month**
- 50 GB data + streaming apps included: around **54,900 COP/month**

**Tigo Prepago:**
- 15 GB data: around **19,900 COP/month**
- 30 GB data + unlimited social: around **29,900 COP/month**
- 60 GB data: around **49,900 COP/month**

**Movistar Prepago:**
- 12 GB data: around **18,900 COP/month**
- 25 GB data: around **32,900 COP/month**

Tigo tends to win on raw data volume at each price point right now. All three carriers offer "unlimited" social and messaging apps (WhatsApp, Instagram, TikTok) even after you exhaust your main data — useful to know, though streaming quality drops once you've burned through your allocation.

Activate packages directly through each carrier's app (Claro Colombia, Mi Tigo, Mi Movistar) or by dialling the short codes they'll give you in store.

## eSIM Options

If you'd rather not deal with a physical SIM at all, eSIM support in Colombia has improved considerably. Both **Claro** and **Tigo** now offer eSIM activation — you'll need to visit an official store with your passport and a compatible device (most iPhone XS and later, Pixel 3 and later, and many Samsung Galaxy S series phones support eSIM).

Alternatively, international eSIM providers like **Airalo**, **Holafly**, and **Nomad** all offer Colombia-specific data packages. Airalo's Colombia eSIM runs roughly 6 USD for 1 GB up to 28 USD for 20 GB — convenient for short trips but poor value compared to local prepaid plans if you're staying more than a couple of weeks. Use them as a bridge to get connected the moment you land, then sort a local SIM within a day or two.

## Home Internet Options

If you're settling in for a month or more, mobile data gets expensive and unreliable for heavy work. Here's the home broadband landscape:

**Claro Fibra** is the most widely available fibre option and reaches most apartment buildings in Bogotá's Chapinero, Usaquén, and La Candelaria areas, and in Medellín's El Poblado, Laureles, and Envigado. Plans start around **59,900 COP/month** for 100 Mbps and go up to **129,900 COP/month** for 500 Mbps. Installation takes 3–7 business days and usually requires a cédula or cédula de extranjería — some landlords will let you add it to an existing account.

**ETB Fibra** is a solid alternative in Bogotá specifically — ETB (Empresa de Telecomunicaciones de Bogotá) is the city's own telecom company and often has slightly cheaper entry-level plans, around **49,900 COP/month** for 100 Mbps. Their customer service is inconsistent but the actual connection quality in central Bogotá is good.

**Tigo Home** is worth comparing if you're in Medellín or Cali — their fibre packages bundle mobile lines and are competitive when you're taking multiple services.

For month-to-month furnished rentals, your landlord will usually have internet included or you can negotiate it in. Most co-working spaces in popular expat neighbourhoods like El Poblado, Laureles, Provenza, and Bogotá's Chicó or Quinta Camacho areas have reliable 100+ Mbps connections if you just need a work base.

## WiFi Calling and VoIP

WhatsApp calls are the default way Colombians communicate — don't fight it, embrace it. You'll use mobile data or WiFi more than actual phone minutes for day-to-day calls. Most prepaid plans include unlimited WhatsApp data even after your main allocation runs out, which covers this.

For calls back home, **WhatsApp** and **Google Meet** handle most use cases. If you need a more professional setup, **Skype** still works fine, and **Zoom** is ubiquitous for work calls. **Google Voice** is an option for US numbers but can be patchy over mobile data — use it over WiFi.

## How to Top Up (Recargar)

Topping up your prepaid balance — *recargar el saldo* — is straightforward:

- **Carrier apps** (Mi Tigo, Claro Colombia, Mi Movistar): easiest method, pay by card or PSE bank transfer
- **Nequi or Daviplata**: Colombia's popular digital wallets let you recharge directly
- **Baloto and Efecty points**: cash top-ups at thousands of convenience stores, pharmacies, and dedicated kiosks. Just ask "¿Me puede hacer una recarga a [carrier]?" and hand over cash. You'll get a confirmation code to dial
- **Éxito and Jumbo supermarkets**: have dedicated recharge counters
- **Oxxo stores**: now widespread in Colombian cities and handle recharges for all three carriers

Minimum top-up is usually around **5,000 COP**, though most packages require at least 19,900 COP to activate.

## Keeping Your Number When Switching Carriers (Portabilidad)

Already have a Colombian number and want to switch operators without losing it? **Portabilidad numérica** (number portability) is your friend. By law, Colombian carriers must process a porting request within 5 business days.

Go to a store of the carrier you want to switch *to* — not from — with your ID and your current number. They handle the request. Your service will have a brief interruption (usually a few hours) during the switch. There's no fee to port your number.

This is worth doing if you're established in Colombia, have given your number to banks, WhatsApp contacts, and businesses, and want to move to a carrier with better coverage or pricing without the hassle of updating everyone.

Getting connected in Colombia is genuinely easy and cheap — there's no excuse for running on roaming rates from home for more than a day. Walk into a Claro or Tigo store, hand over your passport, spend 30,000–50,000 COP on a solid data package, and you're sorted. Everything else you need in this country runs through WhatsApp anyway.
