Let's be honest — Colombian tax law is not exactly a page-turner. But if you're spending serious time here, whether you're working remotely from a flat in Laureles, running a business from El Poblado, or just stretching a long-stay visa into a second year, you need to understand how DIAN (Colombia's tax authority) sees you. Getting this wrong can be expensive. Getting it right is surprisingly manageable.

Here's what you actually need to know.

---

## When Do You Become a Colombian Tax Resident?

The headline rule is the **183-day threshold**. If you spend 183 days or more in Colombia within any 365-day period — not necessarily a calendar year — DIAN considers you a *residente fiscal* (fiscal resident). Those days don't need to be consecutive. A few months here, a visa run to Ecuador, back again — DIAN counts it all.

But the 183-day rule isn't the only trigger. You can also become a tax resident if:

- Your *núcleo familiar* (immediate family — spouse, dependent children) is based in Colombia
- Your primary economic assets or business interests are in Colombia
- You hold Colombian permanent residency (Residencia) status

The practical implication: if you're on a Digital Nomad Visa (DNV) and genuinely hopping between countries, you may stay under the threshold. But many expats who rent a flat, open a bank account, and settle in for a year or two will cross it without realising. Track your days. Seriously — use a spreadsheet or an app like **TravelMath** or just Google Sheets with a simple date log.

## Income Tax Brackets for Residents (2026)

Colombian income tax is calculated in **UVT** (Unidad de Valor Tributario), an indexed unit that adjusts annually for inflation. For 2026, one UVT is worth approximately **49,799 COP** (confirm the exact figure on the DIAN website as this updates each January).

For tax residents, the brackets broadly look like this:

| Taxable Income (UVT) | Approximate Rate |
|---|---|
| 0 – 1,090 UVT | 0% |
| 1,090 – 1,700 UVT | 19% |
| 1,700 – 4,100 UVT | 28% |
| 4,100 – 8,670 UVT | 33% |
| 8,670 – 18,970 UVT | 35% |
| Over 18,970 UVT | 39% |

To put that in real money: the 0% threshold sits at roughly **54,280,000 COP** per year (around USD 13,000–14,000 depending on the exchange rate). If your income is below that, you likely have no income tax obligation — though you may still have a filing obligation, which is a separate question.

Non-residents are taxed at a flat **35%** on Colombian-source income only. No brackets, no deductions — just 35% withheld at source.

## How Foreign-Source Income Is Taxed

This is where things get interesting for digital nomads. If you're a **non-resident** working remotely for foreign clients, Colombia generally does not tax that income. You're earning from abroad, you're not technically a Colombian tax resident, and DIAN isn't particularly concerned with your Upwork invoices.

However, once you become a **tax resident**, the game changes. Colombia taxes residents on their **worldwide income** — meaning your salary from a UK employer, your US client payments, your German freelance gigs — all of it is potentially taxable in Colombia.

There's a practical grace period worth knowing: in your **first year** of becoming a Colombian tax resident (and potentially the second, depending on the specific rules applied), DIAN may only tax you on Colombian-source income. This transitional period is worth discussing with a contador, because it can meaningfully affect your planning.

## The RUT Number — What It Is and How to Get One

The **RUT** (*Registro Único Tributario*) is your tax identification number with DIAN. Think of it like a tax file number or a UTR in the UK context. Almost every financial interaction in Colombia — opening a proper bank account, signing a lease, registering a business — will ask for it at some point.

To get your RUT:

1. Go to the DIAN website (dian.gov.co) and use the online portal, or
2. Visit a DIAN office in person — in Medellín, the main office is on Calle 42 near the Centro Administrativo La Alpujarra; in Bogotá, there are multiple offices including one in Chapinero on Carrera 6

You'll need your passport, your Colombian visa (if applicable), and proof of an address in Colombia (a utility bill or a rental contract usually works). The process is free and typically takes a single visit if your documents are in order. Some expats report the online process being glitchy — especially for foreigners — so going in person is often the more reliable option.

## DIAN Obligations and Annual Filing Deadlines

Not every tax resident is required to file an annual *declaración de renta* (income tax return). You're generally obligated to file if you meet one or more of these thresholds (based on 2025 tax year figures, which carry into 2026 filing):

- Gross income over approximately **1,400 UVT** (~69,700,000 COP)
- Total assets over approximately **4,500 UVT** (~224,000,000 COP)
- Credit/debit card consumption over **1,400 UVT**
- Total purchases and consumption over **1,400 UVT**

The annual filing window typically runs from **August to October**, with the exact dates staggered by the last two digits of your NIT (which is linked to your RUT). Miss your window and the penalties start accumulating immediately.

Filing is done through DIAN's online platform, **Muisca**. It's functional but not exactly intuitive. Most expats who are required to file will want a contador to handle this.

## Tax Treaties — What US, UK, and EU Expats Need to Know

Colombia has a limited number of double taxation treaties (DTTs) compared to other countries. As of 2026, confirmed treaties are in place with a relatively short list including **Spain, Chile, Canada, Mexico, Switzerland, the Czech Republic, India, South Korea, the UK, and Portugal**, among others.

**US citizens** face a uniquely brutal situation. The US taxes its citizens on worldwide income regardless of where they live — so even as a Colombian tax resident, you still file with the IRS. The US-Colombia tax treaty is limited in scope, meaning the **Foreign Tax Credit (Form 1116)** and **Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (Form 2555)** are the primary tools available. Get a US expat tax specialist involved — not just a Colombian contador.

**UK citizens** benefit from the UK-Colombia double taxation agreement, which provides relief mechanisms to avoid being taxed twice on the same income. The practical application still requires careful documentation and, often, professional help coordinating filings in both countries.

**EU citizens** (outside treaty countries) generally have no formal treaty protection. French, Dutch, German, and Italian expats may need to rely on foreign tax credits available in their home country rather than formal treaty relief.

## VAT (IVA) Basics

**IVA** (*Impuesto al Valor Agregado*) is Colombia's VAT. The standard rate is **19%**, which is already built into most prices you pay day-to-day. Some categories are exempt or zero-rated — basic food staples, certain medicines, and public transport, for example.

As an individual expat consumer, IVA is largely invisible to you. Where it becomes relevant is if you're **running a business or freelancing** in Colombia — you may have obligations to register as an IVA collector and charge it on your services, depending on your income levels and business structure. This is another contador conversation.

## Common Deductions and Exemptions

Colombian tax law does allow for certain deductions that can meaningfully reduce your taxable income:

- **25% labour income exemption** — if you receive employment income, 25% of it (up to a cap) is exempt from tax
- **Dependants** — having dependants registered can reduce taxable income
- **Health and pension contributions** — mandatory social security contributions are deductible
- **Mortgage interest** — if you're buying property in Colombia, interest on a Colombian mortgage can be deducted
- **Education expenses** — within limits

The interaction of these deductions with foreign income and foreign-source employment is complicated. Don't guess at this — the numbers matter.

## Crypto Taxation in Colombia

Colombia does not have a dedicated crypto tax law, but DIAN has issued guidance making clear that **cryptocurrency is treated as a taxable asset**. Key points:

- Crypto holdings must be declared as **patrimony (assets)** if you're a filing tax resident
- Gains from selling crypto are taxed as **capital gains** at a flat **15%** for occasional capital gains
- Mining and trading income may be classified differently — as ordinary income — with higher rates applying
- Exchanges operating in Colombia and some P2P transactions are increasingly visible to DIAN

If you're actively trading or holding significant crypto, get specific advice. DIAN's approach to crypto enforcement is tightening.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

DIAN is not particularly aggressive in chasing foreign individuals — until they are. Penalties for late filing start at **5% of the unpaid tax per month**, capped at 100% of the total. Filing with errors or omissions carries its own penalty structure.

More practically, failing to register a RUT when required, or operating a business without proper tax registration, can create problems when you try to open bank accounts, sign contracts, or eventually apply for residency. Colombia's financial system increasingly cross-references DIAN records.

## When to Hire a Contador and What It Costs

A **contador público** (certified public accountant) is your best friend in the Colombian tax system. For straightforward expat situations — filing a single annual return as an employee or freelancer — expect to pay between **300,000 and 800,000 COP** for the service.

More complex situations (business ownership, significant investment income, crypto holdings, multi-country obligations) can run **1,500,000 to 3,000,000 COP** or more.

Finding a good one: ask in expat Facebook groups for Medellín or Bogotá, check the **InterNations** community boards, or ask at coworking spaces like **Selina** or **Atomhouse** in Medellín — expats with accounting needs tend to share referrals. Look for a contador who has **experience with foreign clients** specifically, not just any local accountant.

## Practical Next Steps

If you're spending significant time in Colombia in 2026, here's the short version of what to do:

1. **Track your days** in Colombia from the moment you arrive
2. **Get your RUT** once you have stable residency or if you're starting any kind of income-generating activity
3. **Find a contador** before you think you need one — not after
4. **Check your home country obligations** separately — Colombia sorting you out doesn't mean your home tax authority is happy
5. **Declare crypto** if you're a filing resident — DIAN is paying more attention here

Colombia is genuinely one of the more liveable countries in Latin America for expats. The tax system, once you understand it, is manageable — especially compared to the administrative nightmare some countries put you through. But it does require attention. Don't let it sneak up on you.
