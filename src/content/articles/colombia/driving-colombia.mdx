Driving in Colombia is not for the faint-hearted. It is also, depending on where you are, one of the most rewarding ways to explore the country. The mountain roads through Antioquia, the coffee region between Manizales and Salento, the coast road into Cartagena — genuinely stunning. The roundabout outside Unicentro in Bogotá at 8am on a Monday? Less so. This guide gives you the honest, practical version of what you need to know before you get behind the wheel.

## Can You Drive on a Foreign Licence?

Yes — for a while. Foreign nationals can legally drive in Colombia using a valid licence from their home country, provided it has not expired. Technically you should also carry an International Driving Permit (IDP) alongside your national licence, though in practice enforcement is inconsistent and many expats drive for months without one. If you are pulled over by *tránsito* (traffic police), a valid foreign licence is generally accepted.

The catch is time. Once you have a *cédula de extranjería* (foreigner ID card) — which most legal residents will have — you are expected to obtain a Colombian driving licence. Driving on a foreign licence after you are officially resident is a grey area, and if you are involved in an accident or a serious stop, it can create real complications with your insurance claim or liability.

Practically speaking: if you are in Colombia on a tourist visa or short-stay, your foreign licence is fine. If you are resident, get the Colombian licence sorted.

## Getting a Colombian Driving Licence

The process is handled through the *Superintendencia de Transporte* and authorised *centros de reconocimiento de conductores* (CRC). It is more involved than many countries but straightforward if you know the steps.

**What you need:**
- Valid *cédula de extranjería* or Colombian ID
- Medical and psychophysical exam at a certified CRC (expect to pay 80,000–120,000 COP)
- Theory test (*examen de conocimientos*) — covers road signs, rules, and the traffic code
- Practical driving test if you do not hold a valid foreign licence
- Payment of the licence fee: roughly 150,000–200,000 COP depending on the category

In Bogotá, you can book the exams through the *Secretaría Distrital de Movilidad* portal. In Medellín, the process goes through *Área Metropolitana*. The CRCs handle the medical portion; licences are issued at authorised *organismos de tránsito*. Bring your foreign licence — it can sometimes exempt you from the practical test, though this is at the examiner's discretion.

Category B covers regular passenger vehicles. Category A covers motorcycles, which are their own world in Colombia.

## Pico y Placa — The Plate Restriction System

*Pico y placa* (literally "peak and plate") is a traffic restriction scheme that limits which vehicles can be on the road during peak hours based on the last digit of their number plate. It applies in Bogotá, Medellín, and Cali, with different rules in each city. Getting caught violating it results in a fine of around 490,000 COP and points on your licence.

**Bogotá:** Restrictions apply Monday to Friday, typically 6am–9pm (check current times as these shift). Two plate-ending digits are restricted per day, rotating in a cycle. Check the Movilidad Bogotá website or the *Movilidad Bogotá* app before you drive.

**Medellín:** Medellín's scheme is stricter during peak hours (roughly 7am–9am and 5pm–8pm on weekdays) but less restrictive mid-day. Use the *Movilidad Medellín* app or check the Área Metropolitana website.

**Cali:** Similar rotating system. The *MiCali* app is the local go-to.

If you are renting a car, ask specifically what plate digit it ends in before you book — some rental companies will not mention this upfront, and you do not want to get fined in your first week.

## SOAT — Mandatory Vehicle Insurance

*SOAT* (*Seguro Obligatorio de Accidentes de Tránsito*) is Colombia's compulsory third-party insurance and every vehicle on the road must have a valid one. It covers medical expenses and death benefits for accident victims — it is not comprehensive insurance and does not cover vehicle damage.

You can buy SOAT from major insurers including Sura, Bolívar, and Allianz, either online or at authorised points of sale. Many *droguerías* (pharmacies) like Drogas La Rebaja also sell them. Costs vary by vehicle type and model year:

- Small passenger car (e.g. Renault Sandero, Chevrolet Spark): roughly 280,000–380,000 COP per year
- Motorcycles: 200,000–300,000 COP depending on engine size

Driving without SOAT is a serious offence — your vehicle can be seized. When you buy or rent a car, verify the SOAT is current. The expiry date is printed on the document and also searchable on the RUNT (*Registro Único Nacional de Tránsito*) website using the plate number.

## Tecnomecánica — Vehicle Roadworthiness Inspection

If a vehicle is more than two years old, it requires a *tecnomecánica* — a roadworthiness inspection carried out at authorised centres called CDAs (*Centros de Diagnóstico Automotor*). The frequency depends on the vehicle's age:

- 2–6 years old: every two years
- Older than 6 years: annually

A typical tecnomecánica costs 90,000–160,000 COP for a passenger car. Centres are widespread — in Bogotá you will find CDAs in areas like Puente Aranda, Fontibón, and along Avenida Boyacá. Driving with an expired tecnomecánica results in fines and potential impoundment.

## Buying vs Renting a Car

**Renting** is straightforward for short stays. Major operators like Europcar, Hertz, and local firm Localiza have desks at Bogotá's El Dorado airport and in city centres. Daily rates for a basic hatchback start around 120,000–180,000 COP per day, rising sharply if you want full insurance coverage. Mileage caps are common — read the contract carefully.

**Buying** a vehicle is a bigger commitment but worth it for long-term residents. The process involves a *traspaso* (ownership transfer), done through a notary and registered with RUNT. This is where you need to be careful: always verify a vehicle's legal status on RUNT before purchase (using the plate or chassis number) — you can check for outstanding fines, whether it has been reported stolen, and whether the tecnomecánica and SOAT are current.

A used Chevrolet Spark (the workhorse of Colombian city driving) in reasonable condition runs 25,000,000–40,000,000 COP. A Renault Logan, popular with families, starts around 35,000,000–50,000,000 COP used. Factor in the traspaso cost (roughly 300,000–600,000 COP in notary and registry fees) and a mechanical check from an independent *mecánico* before you commit.

## Road Conditions and Driving Culture

Let's be direct: Colombian driving culture is aggressive by most international standards. Lane discipline is loose, indicators are used sparingly, and motorcycles treat lanes as suggestions. In Bogotá, expect gridlock on major arteries like the Calle 80, Avenida El Dorado, and the Autopista Norte during rush hours. In Medellín, the Avenida El Poblado and the tunnel exits near El Centro back up badly.

Interurban roads (*carreteras*) range from excellent four-lane highways to narrow two-lane mountain roads where lorries and buses will not slow down for you. The Bogotá–Medellín road via La Manuela has dramatic scenery and genuine drop-offs. Drive in daylight where possible and do not rush mountain roads.

*Policía de carretera* (highway police) checkpoints are common. Always carry your documents.

## Toll Roads (Peajes) and How to Pay

Colombia's main highways have frequent *peajes* (tolls), usually every 50–80km. Most toll plazas are cash only, though some accept the *Tag* electronic transponder system. A typical toll for a passenger car costs 12,000–25,000 COP per plaza.

Always carry small bills — 5,000 and 10,000 COP notes — because toll booth attendants regularly run out of change. On major routes like the Bogotá–Villavicencio or the coffee region highway, budget for several peajes each direction.

## What to Do If Stopped by Police (Tránsito)

Stay calm. Colombian traffic police (*tránsito*) have a reputation — sometimes deserved — for looking for irregular payments. Your rights: you are entitled to a formal receipt (*comparendo*) for any fine. If an officer suggests sorting it out *de manera informal*, politely decline and ask for the official ticket. Keep your documents organised and accessible: licence, SOAT, tecnomecánica, and ownership papers (*tarjeta de propiedad*).

Do not get confrontational. If you believe a fine is unjust, you can contest it through the *Secretaría de Movilidad* in writing.

## Fuel Costs and Types

Petrol stations (*estaciones de gasolina*) in Colombia sell *corriente* (regular, 87 octane) and *extra* (premium, 95 octane). As of early 2026, corriente runs approximately 13,500–14,500 COP per litre in most cities, slightly higher in remote areas. Extra is around 15,500–17,000 COP per litre. Many stations also sell *ACPM* (diesel) at roughly 10,500–12,000 COP per litre, and natural gas (*gas natural vehicular*) is common on taxis.

Most modern cars in Colombia run on corriente fine unless the manufacturer specifies otherwise.

## Parking — Apps, Lots, and Cuidacarros

Street parking in Bogotá and Medellín is a mixture of marked paid zones, underground *parqueaderos* (car parks), and the informal *cuidacarros* economy. A *cuidacarros* is someone — often an older man or a young lad — who watches your car on the street and expects a tip of 1,000–2,000 COP when you return. It is informal, largely harmless, and fairly universal.

For finding legal parking, Waze is useful for spotting *parqueaderos* nearby. In Bogotá's Chapinero, Zona Rosa, and Usaquén, paid street parking zones are marked in blue — pay at the nearby kiosk or via the *Parqueaderos Bogotá* app. Underground car parks in commercial areas charge roughly 3,000–6,000 COP per half hour.

## Ride-Hailing Alternatives

If driving is not for you, the app-based options in Colombia are worth knowing. **Uber** operates in a legally ambiguous space — it has done for years — but continues to function in most major cities. **DiDi** launched strongly and is now the dominant platform in many cities, with competitive prices. **InDriver** works on a negotiated fare model and is popular in secondary cities and for longer trips.

Traditional yellow *taxis* are metered (*taxímetro*) in Bogotá; the app **TaxiBeat** or **Tappsi** can help you book one without flagging down a stranger. In Medellín, the metro plus the *Encicla* bike-share system can actually be faster than driving during peak hours.

## Road Trip Tips and Best Driving Routes

The best driving in Colombia is not in the cities — it is between them.

**Bogotá to Villa de Leyva** (3 hours via Tunja): Colonial roads, light traffic outside weekends, easily one of the most rewarding day trips from the capital.

**Medellín to Guatapé**: About 1.5 hours on a good road. Leave before 8am on weekends to beat the traffic.

**Manizales to Salento through the coffee region**: Genuinely spectacular mountain driving — slow, winding, and worth every minute. Allow twice what Google Maps suggests.

**Bogotá to Cartagena via the Atlantic coast**: A multi-day overland route that most people fly, but if you have time, the road through Barrancabermeja and Mompox is an education in Colombian geography.

Download offline maps on **Maps.me** or Google Maps before heading into mountain areas — signal drops without warning, and Colombian GPS addresses are not always logical.

Keep your tank full on rural routes, carry water and a basic toolkit, and check road conditions on the **INVIAS** website (invias.gov.co) before heading out on long trips — landslides close key roads regularly, especially during rainy season (April–May and October–November).

Driving in Colombia demands patience and adaptability. But get comfortable with the chaos, learn your plate restrictions, and keep your documents current — and the roads here will take you somewhere genuinely worth going.
