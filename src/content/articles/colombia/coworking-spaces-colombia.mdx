Colombia has quietly become one of the best places in the world to work remotely. The time zones are friendly (you're synced with the US East Coast), the coffee is exceptional, and the cost of living means your dollars or euros go a long way. But finding a coworking space that actually delivers — reliable fibre, good air conditioning, and people worth talking to — takes a bit of local knowledge.

This guide cuts through the noise. Whether you're passing through Cartagena for a couple of weeks or settling into Medellín for six months, here's where to set up your laptop in 2026.

---

## Medellín: The Digital Nomad Capital of Colombia

Medellín has more coworking options per square kilometre than anywhere else in the country. The two main neighbourhoods for remote workers are **El Poblado** (polished, expensive, expat-heavy) and **Laureles** (calmer, more Colombian, better value). Neither is wrong — it depends on whether you want rooftop networking events or a quiet desk near a good bakery.

### Selina Medellín (El Poblado)

Selina is the global coliving brand that planted its flag firmly in El Poblado, and the Medellín outpost is one of its stronger locations. The coworking floor is well-lit, has a mix of hot desks and private pods, and the Wi-Fi consistently clocks in at 80–120 Mbps. The vibe is social — there are always people here between Zoom calls, and the communal areas blur into the hostel common spaces.

**Rates:** Day pass around **45,000–55,000 COP**, monthly desk from **700,000–900,000 COP** depending on the package. Book online for occasional discounts.

**Best for:** People who want to combine work and social life. Not ideal if you need silence.

### Tinkko (El Poblado and Laureles)

Tinkko is a Colombian-born chain that's expanded aggressively over the past few years, and it's arguably the most consistent coworking experience in the country. The El Poblado branch is sleek and corporate-feeling; the Laureles location (on Avenida El Poblado near the Circular area) is slightly more relaxed. Both have solid infrastructure: 200+ Mbps fibre, good ergonomic chairs, private phone booths, and meeting rooms you can book by the hour.

**Rates:** Day pass **35,000–40,000 COP**, weekly pass around **120,000 COP**, monthly from **450,000–600,000 COP** for a hot desk. Dedicated desks and private offices go higher.

**Best for:** People who need reliable infrastructure and a professional environment. The monthly plans are genuinely good value.

### WeWork Medellín (El Centro and El Poblado)

WeWork operates in Medellín with a couple of locations, including a prominent one in the Torre Coltejer area of El Centro. If you're used to WeWork in London or New York, you'll feel immediately at home — same aesthetic, same app-based booking, same kombucha-on-tap energy. Internet is fast (100–200 Mbps), the facilities are excellent, and the private offices are the best option for small remote teams.

**Rates:** Hot desk day passes hover around **60,000–80,000 COP**. Monthly memberships start at roughly **900,000 COP** but can climb quickly with add-ons.

**Best for:** Corporate freelancers, startup teams, anyone expensing it.

### Loft Coworking (Laureles)

Loft is a local favourite that doesn't get enough attention in the expat forums. Tucked into Laureles — specifically around the Calle 33 area — it has a quieter, more focused atmosphere than the El Poblado options. The community is a mix of Colombian entrepreneurs and longer-stay foreigners who've figured out that Laureles is the better neighbourhood to actually live in.

**Rates:** Day passes from **25,000 COP**, monthly hot desks from **380,000 COP**. One of the better-value options in the city.

**Best for:** If you want to actually integrate with the local startup scene and not just talk to other nomads.

### Epicentro (El Poblado)

Epicentro positions itself as a premium coworking and events space, and it earns that label. The design is impressive — exposed concrete, lots of natural light — and the meeting room facilities are top-tier. It's popular with Colombian businesses as well as international remote workers, so the networking potential is real. Located near Parque El Poblado.

**Rates:** Day passes around **50,000 COP**, monthly from **650,000 COP**.

## Bogotá: Working in the Capital

Bogotá is enormous and the quality of coworking varies wildly by neighbourhood. Stick to **Chapinero** (the creative and startup district, roughly between Calles 57 and 72) or **Usaquén** (quieter, more residential, good coffee shops) and you'll be fine.

### WeWork Bogotá (Chapinero and Chicó)

WeWork has multiple Bogotá locations, including well-regarded spaces in the Chicó area and Chapinero Alto. The Chicó location near Carrera 7 is particularly well-situated — accessible, with good nearby lunch spots. Prices are slightly higher than Medellín.

**Rates:** Hot desks from **70,000–90,000 COP** per day, monthly from **1,000,000–1,300,000 COP**.

### Tinkko Bogotá (Chapinero)

The Chapinero Tinkko is a reliable workhorse. Nothing flashy, but consistently good internet, decent coffee included, and a professional crowd. If you're in Bogotá for a week and need to actually ship work, this is your safe bet.

**Rates:** Day pass **40,000 COP**, monthly hot desk from **550,000 COP**.

### Workia (Usaquén and Chapinero)

Workia is a Bogotá-native brand that's built a quiet reputation for quality. The Usaquén location is particularly pleasant — the neighbourhood has a village-within-a-city feel and the space reflects that. Good natural light, a friendly community manager, reliable 150 Mbps fibre.

**Rates:** Day passes from **35,000 COP**, monthly memberships from **500,000 COP**.

**Best for:** Longer stays in Bogotá; the monthly rates are competitive and the environment is calm.

## Cartagena: Working Where It's Hot

Working in Cartagena requires making peace with the heat. Air conditioning is non-negotiable — check before you book anything.

### Selina Cartagena (Getsemaní)

Selina's Cartagena location is set in a restored colonial building in Getsemaní, the neighbourhood that's become the creative heart of the city. The coworking area is on the smaller side but well air-conditioned, and the rooftop pool being 30 seconds away is either a productivity killer or a mental health essential, depending on your disposition.

**Rates:** Day pass **50,000–60,000 COP**, monthly from **800,000 COP**.

### Medelab Cartagena (Bocagrande)

Despite the name (a nod to Medellín's innovation culture), Medelab operates out of Bocagrande — the modern, high-rise district across the bay from the walled city. Less romantic than Getsemaní, but the infrastructure is better. Faster internet (200+ Mbps), fewer power cuts, better suited to serious work.

**Rates:** Day passes around **40,000 COP**, monthly from **550,000 COP**.

**Best for:** Longer Cartagena stays where the colonial charm has worn off and you just need reliable fibre.

## Santa Marta, Cali, Barranquilla, and Bucaramanga

### Santa Marta

The coworking scene here is thinner. **El Rodadero** has a handful of informal spaces attached to hostels — ask at any of the beachside accommodations. The **Centro Histórico** has more character and a couple of café-coworking hybrids worth trying. Expect day passes in the **20,000–35,000 COP** range. Internet quality is the biggest variable — always test before committing.

### Cali

Cali's remote work infrastructure has improved significantly. The **Ciudad Jardín** and **Granada** neighbourhoods have the most options, including local chains and independent spaces. Look for **Atom House** and **La Maquinaria** — both have solid reputations with the local tech community. Prices are among the lowest in the country: day passes from **20,000–30,000 COP**, monthly from **300,000–400,000 COP**.

### Barranquilla

**Barranquilla Coworking** in the Prado neighbourhood is the most established option in a city that doesn't get much nomad traffic. If you're here for the Carnaval or transiting to the coast, it's a decent fallback. Monthly rates from **400,000 COP**.

### Bucaramanga

A surprisingly underrated city for remote work. **Cabecera** is the neighbourhood to be in — there are a few well-run independent coworking spaces here, and the city's lower cost of living makes the economics attractive. Expect monthly rates around **350,000–500,000 COP**.

## Café Alternatives: The Unofficial Coworking Circuit

Sometimes you don't need a desk, you need a good flat white and three hours of uninterrupted focus. Colombia's café culture delivers. In Medellín, **Pergamino** (El Poblado) and **Colo Café** (Laureles) both tolerate laptop workers for hours if you keep ordering. In Bogotá, the Chapinero café scene around Calle 69 is dense and reliable. In Cartagena, stay out of the tourist traps in the walled city — the Getsemaní options are better and cheaper.

The rule of thumb: buy something every 90 minutes, don't hog a table during lunch rush, and always ask about Wi-Fi before you settle in.

## Coliving + Coworking Combos

If you're arriving without accommodation sorted, coliving-coworking bundles make sense for the first few weeks. **Selina** operates this model across Medellín, Bogotá, and Cartagena. **Nómada Medellín** and **Casa Laureles** are smaller, more local alternatives in Medellín's Laureles that combine a room and desk in a single monthly rate — often landing between **2,200,000–3,500,000 COP** all-in, which is competitive once you factor in utilities and not having to negotiate a lease.

---

## Home Internet as an Alternative

If you're staying somewhere for more than a month, setting up home internet is worth considering. **Claro**, **ETB** (Bogotá), and **Tigo** are the main providers. A decent residential fibre plan runs **80,000–130,000 COP** per month for 100–300 Mbps. Installation can take 5–10 days and requires a local address, but once it's up, it's reliable enough that many longer-stay expats ditch coworking entirely.

The catch: apartments in Colombia don't always include internet, and landlords on short-term contracts sometimes won't allow installation. Worth checking before you sign anything.

## What to Actually Look for When Choosing a Space

Skip the aesthetic photos and ask these questions:

- **What's the actual internet speed?** Ask for a speed test result, or run one yourself on arrival. Anything below 50 Mbps for uploads is going to cause problems on video calls.
- **Is there backup power?** Power cuts (*apagones*) happen. The better spaces have UPS systems or generators.
- **What are the real hours?** Some spaces advertise 24/7 access but in practice lock up at 8pm.
- **Is there air conditioning?** Critical in Cartagena and Santa Marta. Less so in Medellín and Bogotá, where the climate does the work.
- **What's the noise situation?** Open-plan spaces in social coworking spots can be genuinely loud. If you're on calls all day, look for phone booths or private rooms.
- **Monthly vs day rate value?** If you're staying more than 10 days, the monthly rate almost always pays for itself. Do the maths before you commit to daily passes.

Colombia rewards the remote worker who takes a bit of time to get set up properly. The infrastructure is there — you just need to know where to look.
